B1 — Data Protection Notices
Singapore
Singapore's principal data protection statute is the Personal Data Protection Act 2012 (PDPA),
administered by the Personal Data Protection Commission (PDPC). The PDPA applies
extraterritorially to any organisation that collects, uses, or discloses personal data of
individuals in Singapore, regardless of whether the organisation has a physical presence in
the country.
Obligation
Summary
Consent
Personal data may not be collected, used, or disclosed unless the individual has given consent for those specific purposes
Purpose Limitation
Data may be used only for purposes that a reasonable person would consider appropriate
Notification
Individuals must be informed of the purposes for which their data is being collected, used, or disclosed
Access and Correction
Individuals have the right to access and correct their personal data
Protection
Reasonable security arrangements must be made to prevent unauthorised access, collection, use, disclosure, copying, modification, disposal or similar risks
Retention Limitation
Personal data must not be retained longer than necessary
Transfer Limitation
Personal data may not be transferred to a jurisdiction outside Singapore unless the recipient is bound by legally enforceable obligations providing a comparable standard of protection
Breach Notification
Mandatory notification of data breaches to the PDPC and affected individuals is required
Accountability
Organisations are responsible for complying with the PDPA and for any third-party processing of personal data on their behalf
Non-compliance may result in financial penalties of up to SGD 1 million or 10% of annual
turnover, whichever is higher.
The PDPA is supplemented by subsidiary regulations, including the Personal Data Protection
Regulations 2021 (addressing data transfers out of Singapore and procedures relating to
access and correction requests) and the Personal Data Protection (Notification of Data
Breaches) Regulations 2021 (prescribing thresholds and timelines for mandatory breach
notification).
Licensee responsibility: If Licensee's use of the CAS Architecture involves the processing
of personal data of individuals in Singapore, Licensee must ensure compliance with the PDPA.
The CAS Architecture is an architectural specification and does not itself collect or process
personal data; compliance obligations arise from Licensee's own data processing activities.
Japan
Japan's principal data protection legislation is the Act on the Protection of Personal
Information (APPI), which applies to any business operator handling personal information of
individuals in Japan, including foreign companies that provide goods or services to individuals
in Japan or monitor their behaviour. The Personal Information Protection Commission (PPC)
serves as Japan's independent regulatory authority, with enforcement powers ranging from
administrative guidance to criminal penalties.
Requirement
Summary
Purpose Specification and Limitation
The purpose of personal information use must be specified and limited to that purpose
Consent Rules
Explicit consent is generally required, with certain exceptions
Security Control Obligations
Handling operators must take necessary and appropriate action for security control over personal data they handle
Third-Party Transfer Restrictions
Transfer of personal data to third parties (including foreign operators) requires prior consent of the data subject
Extraterritorial Application
Foreign business operators handling personal information of individuals in Japan are subject to the same obligations as Japanese entities (Article 171 of the APPI)
Breach Notification
Obligations to notify the PPC and affected individuals of data breaches
Administrative Surcharges
2025–2026 reforms introduce administrative surcharges for serious violations
The Japanese government has approved amendments to the APPI to ease data use for AI
development, allowing data to be collected and used without consent where data is
pseudonymised and for AI development purposes that do not identify individuals. These
amendments are currently progressing through the legislative process, with proposed
implementation expected in 2026–2027.
Licensee responsibility: Licensees handling personal information of individuals in Japan
must comply with the APPI, including its extraterritorial provisions. The CAS Architecture
itself does not process personal data; compliance obligations arise solely from Licensee's
own activities.
South Korea
South Korea's Personal Information Protection Act (PIPA) is the general law governing the
protection of personal information. The PIPA was amended on 10 March 2026, with most
provisions taking effect on 11 September 2026. The Personal Information Protection
Commission (PIPC) is the primary enforcement authority.
Change
Summary
Increased Administrative Penalties
Maximum penalty raised from 3% to 10% of total revenue in specific circumstances, including repeated violations involving willful misconduct or gross negligence within a three-year period, violations affecting 10 million or more data subjects, or failure to comply with a corrective order issued by the PIPC
Expanded Breach Notification
The definition of "breach" now includes forgery, alteration, or damage of personal information; notification is required upon becoming aware of a "possibility of a breach", moving the trigger to an earlier stage
CEO Accountability
The business owner or representative is explicitly designated as the "ultimate person responsible for the processing and protection of personal information"
Mandatory ISMS-P Certification
The Personal Information & Information Security Management System certification, previously voluntary, is now mandatory for private entities meeting specific criteria (effective 1 July 2027)
The PIPA has extraterritorial application and applies to foreign entities that process personal
information of individuals in South Korea.
Licensee responsibility: Licensees processing personal information of individuals in South
Korea must comply with the PIPA. The CAS Architecture is a specification, not a data
processing system; compliance obligations arise from Licensee's own processing activities.
India
India's data protection framework is governed by the Digital Personal Data Protection Act
2023 (DPDP Act) and the Digital Personal Data Protection Rules 2025 (DPDP Rules), which
together form India's comprehensive personal data protection regime. The Rules were notified
on 14 November 2025 and have a staggered enforcement timeline.
Feature
Summary
Consent Managers
Registered entities acting as a single point of contact for data principals to give, manage, review, and withdraw consent
Consent Notice Requirements
Notices must be clear, accessible, and separate from other information, containing an itemised list of personal data being processed and the specific purposes
Data Protection Board of India
Established as the regulatory authority with power to register or suspend consent managers and investigate data breaches
Cross-Border Transfers
The DPDP Act allows free transferability of personal data to any country or territory outside India
Operational Requirements
Include security safeguards, breach reporting, retention, children's data, and significant data fiduciary obligations, with an 18-month transition period (effective May 2027)
Data Principal Rights
Include the right to withdraw consent, correct and erase personal data, and redressal against misuse
India currently has no dedicated, comprehensive AI-specific legislation, with AI oversight
emerging from the Information Technology Act 2000, the DPDP Act, and recent February 2026
amendments to IT Intermediary Rules targeting synthetically generated content.
Licensee responsibility: Licensees processing digital personal data of individuals in India
must comply with the DPDP Act and DPDP Rules. The CAS Architecture is an architectural
specification; compliance obligations arise from Licensee's own data processing activities.
China / PRC
China's personal information protection regime is governed by the Personal Information
Protection Law (PIPL), the Data Security Law (DSL), and the Cybersecurity Law (CSL), which
form an integrated data governance system. The Cyberspace Administration of China (CAC)
is the primary regulatory authority.
Provision
Summary
Scope
Applies to the processing of personal information of individuals within China, as well as extraterritorial processing where activities aim to provide products or services to individuals in China or analyse or assess their behaviour
Sensitive Personal Information
Biometric data, financial account information, precise location tracking, health data, and personal information of minors under 14 are subject to stricter protection and separate consent requirements
Cross-Border Data Transfer
Three primary mechanisms: security assessment (default for most commercial transfers), standard contractual clauses (available in limited circumstances), and personal information protection certification (available in limited circumstances)
Personal Information Protection Officer
Processors meeting CAC-specified thresholds must designate a PIPO
Impact Assessments
Required for certain processing activities, including cross-border transfers
Penalties
Fines of up to RMB 50 million or 5% of previous year's revenue for serious violations
For most commercial cross-border transfers of personal information from China, the CAC
security assessment is the primary mechanism, with standard contractual clauses and
certification available only in limited circumstances.
The CSL was amended effective 1 January 2026, promoting AI research and development
while strengthening enforcement mechanisms. China continues to develop its AI governance
framework, with draft rules on interactive AI services and trial guidelines on AI ethics review
being issued in early 2026.

process personal data; compliance obligations arise from Licensee's own activities.
Cross-border transfer of personal information from China requires compliance with one of the
three statutory mechanisms under the PIPL, with the CAC security assessment being the
default for most commercial transfers.
B2 — AI Regulatory Landscape Notices
Singapore
Singapore has positioned itself as a leader in AI governance. In January 2026, the Infocomm
Media Development Authority (IMDA) released the Model AI Governance Framework for
Agentic AI at the World Economic Forum in Davos — the world's first framework specifically
addressing agentic AI systems. The framework provides guidance on managing risks in
deployment of agentic AI, covering technical and non-technical measures while emphasising
human accountability. In February 2026, Prime Minister Lawrence Wong announced the
establishment of a new National AI Council to provide strategic direction and drive
Singapore's AI agenda.
The Model Agentic AI Framework is non-binding guidance, not legislation. Licensees deploying
CAS in AI systems within Singapore should familiarise themselves with this framework as an
indicator of emerging best practices and regulatory expectations.
Japan
Japan has articulated an ambitious policy goal of making Japan "the world's most AI-friendly
country for development and deployment", seeking to achieve "trustworthy AI" by balancing
innovation promotion with appropriate risk management. The Japanese government has
approved amendments to the APPI to ease data use for AI development, allowing data to be
collected and used without consent where data is pseudonymised and for AI development
purposes that do not identify individuals. The amendments are currently progressing through
the legislative process.
Rather than enacting a dedicated AI law, Japan is pursuing a light-touch, principles-based
approach, relying on existing frameworks (APPI, guidelines, industry self-regulation) while
selectively relaxing data protection rules to promote AI innovation. Licensees using CAS in AI
systems in Japan should monitor ongoing legislative developments.
South Korea
South Korea enacted the Framework Act on the Development of Artificial Intelligence and
Establishment of Trust (AI Basic Act), which took effect on 22 January 2026. This is the
world's first comprehensive AI law combining AI governance, industrial policy, and
risk-management obligations into a single statutory framework. The Act adopts a flexible,
principles-led approach, aiming to promote AI development while setting minimum safeguards.
It applies extraterritorially to overseas activities affecting Korean markets or users, though AI
developed solely for national defense purposes is exempt.
Licensees deploying CAS in AI systems within South Korea or affecting Korean users should
ensure compliance with the AI Basic Act.
India
India has not enacted dedicated, comprehensive AI-specific legislation as of February 2026.
AI governance emerges from the Information Technology Act 2000, the DPDP Act, and the
Information Technology (Intermediary Guidelines and Digital Media Ethics Code) Rules 2026
(IT Amendment Rules), which took effect on 20 February 2026. These amendments require:
Mandatory labelling of AI-generated and synthetically altered content
Takedown of unlawful AI-generated content within three hours
Expanded intermediary liability for AI-generated content classified as "synthetically
generated information"
Licensees deploying CAS in AI systems accessible in India must comply with these
intermediary obligations.
China
China continues to develop its AI governance framework through an agile, layered approach,
seeking to balance technological innovation with data security, privacy protection, and
intellectual property rights.

B3 — Export Control Notices
Singapore
Singapore requires permits or registrations for the export, transhipment, transit, or brokering
of strategic goods and related technology listed in the Strategic Goods Control List. The
Strategic Trade Scheme (STS) streamlines permit procedures and supports internal export
control compliance. The Strategic Goods (Control) Act (SGCA) is the primary legislation.
Licensees exporting CAS-related technology from Singapore must ensure compliance with
the SGCA and the STS.
Japan
Japan continues to update its export control regulations in alignment with international
regimes, with key changes rolling out through February 2026 addressing risks tied to critical
and emerging technologies. Notably, China has imposed export controls on dual-use items
destined for Japan effective 6 January 2026, with immediate effect and no wind-down period.
Licensees involved in cross-border transfers of CAS-related technology to or from Japan must
monitor these developments.
South Korea
South Korea continues to implement its export control framework under the Foreign Trade Act.
In 2026, the government has announced emergency supply-demand adjustment measures,
including export controls, to stabilise domestic supply of certain goods. The AI Basic Act
applies to overseas activities affecting Korean markets or users, potentially impacting export
of AI-related technologies.
Licensees transferring CAS-related technology to or from South Korea should ensure
compliance with applicable export control regulations.
India
India's export control framework is governed by the Foreign Exchange Management (Export
and Import of Goods and Services) Regulations 2026 (FEMA 2026), effective 1 October 2026,
superseding the 2015 regulations. India also operates the SCOMET framework (Special
Chemicals, Organisms, Materials, Equipment and Technologies) for the export of dual-use
and sensitive goods and technologies.
Licensees exporting CAS-related technology from India must comply with FEMA 2026 and
the SCOMET framework.

China has significantly strengthened its export control framework, particularly for dual-use
